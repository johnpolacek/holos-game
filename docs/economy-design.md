# HOLOS
### Economy Design — The Price of Purpose

*How Holos prices what a civilization does. There is no money. Every choice
is paid in Energy, Matter, Compute, and real time — felt afterward in
Signature and Coherence — and the bill is always attached to a purpose,
never to a chore.*

---

## About this document

The [vision](./vision.md) says what Holos is. The [Act 2](./act2-design.md)
and [Act 3](./act3-design.md) designs specify the two strategy acts. This
document sits across both and pins the one thing they describe in shape but
not in detail: the **choice economy** — how a player spends resources and
time, and where exclusivity comes from. [playstyles.md](./playstyles.md)'s
first neutrality rule — *prices, not verdicts* — is the stance; this is that
stance costed out.

Two standing rules for everything below.

- **Relative, never numeric.** This document names **cost classes** and
  **rhythm targets**, never final numbers. "A probe launch is routine
  income; a relativistic ship is an era's savings" is the kind of statement
  it makes. Tuning happens in play.
- **Where this and the vision disagree, the vision wins**, and where a
  mechanic here would contradict an act design, the act design's behavior
  is canonical and this document is the account of its economics.

---

## The one principle: price purposes, never logistics

The [altitude principle](./act2-design.md) is absolute, and the economy is
where it is most easily broken. The lowest level of decision the game ever
prices is *which purpose* — swarm the star, send an envoy, go dark, launch
a child. It **never** prices a logistics sub-task. Routing matter,
scheduling collectors, staffing a mission, balancing a colony's books: all
of that is the mind's job, executed flawlessly and off-screen, and none of
it is ever a line item the player pays.

So the economy charges for **the what** and lets the mind own **the how**.
A cost profile hangs off a purpose the way a price hangs off a decision to
buy, and the player never sees the supply chain behind the price any more
than a shopper sees the freight behind a shelf. This is the test every
priced action must pass: if paying it would require the player to think
about *how* rather than *whether*, the price is at the wrong altitude and
gets moved up.

## No money

There is no universal medium of exchange — no credits, no gold, no abstract
ledger that stores value and moves it around at will. Money would quietly
undo the whole design: it would let a civilization pool wealth, buy its way
past physics, and treat a star and a secret as the same kind of thing
measured on one scale. Holos refuses all three. Value does not abstract; it
stays in the physical and informational form it was won in, and it is spent
in that form or not at all.

What a purpose costs is a **cost profile** in these terms:

| Currency | What it is | What it prices |
|---|---|---|
| **Energy** | Power harnessed so far; the master input. | Everything, ultimately — and *bright* work most of all. The energy ladder is the account this draws down. |
| **Matter** | The local mass budget. | Construction, especially bright construction — swarms, structures, the bodies of ships and agents. |
| **Compute** | Grown from Energy; the price of **knowing**. | Simulation, analysis, decoding, classification, research, and training or instantiating a mind. You pay Compute to *find out* and to *think at scale*. |
| **Real time** | The wall-clock a project or flight runs on. | The one price the others cannot buy down. Deep-time projects complete on real async clocks; interstellar flight and light-lag charge years no budget can shorten. |

And two things that are **not paid at the counter** but are left behind by
what you paid:

| | What it is | How it is left |
|---|---|---|
| **Signature** | The residue — the hidden fourth cost of bright work. | Bright, Energy-heavy spending marks the sky. You never pay it; it accrues, and comes due only once the galaxy is watching (Act 3). See *Signature* below. |
| **Coherence** | A meter of the mind's unity, not a pool. | Nothing spends it. Some spending *injures* it. See *Coherence* below. |

The clean way to say it: **Energy, Matter, Compute, and real time are the
prices; Signature and Coherence are what the prices do to you.**

## The cost profile — one shape for everything

Every project, mission, launch, and directive carries the same object: a
cost profile across the four prices, plus its Signature residue and its
Coherence risk. One shape, priced differently, is what makes the whole game
legible — a swarm stage and an envoy and a forced directive are all read
the same way.

| Purpose | Energy | Matter | Compute | Time | Signature | Coherence | Class |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Launch a probe / seedship | low | low–mod | low | long *(flight)* | faint | — | Ambient |
| Sharpen an instrument, run a survey | low | low | high | short–mod | — | — | Investment |
| A swarm stage / star-lifting | high | high | mod | long | adds a lot | — | Endeavor |
| The compute heart (dark) | mod | mod | high | long | sheds | heals | Endeavor |
| The deep Vault (dark) | mod | high | mod | very long | sheds | heals | Endeavor |
| Send an envoy (mission) | mod | mod | high *(instantiate)* | long *(transit)* | by method | risk at scale | Investment–Endeavor |
| Transmit a fork to a receiver | mod | — | high *(encode)* | = light-distance | a bright beam | risk *(One Mind)* | Investment–Endeavor |
| A relativistic ship | very high | high | mod | huge | adds a lot | low | Epochal |
| Force a directive against nature | — | — | — | immediate | — | wounds | Epochal *(self-harm)* |

Read the columns, not the cells: a *bright* purpose costs Energy and Matter
and shines; a *knowing* purpose costs Compute; a *dark* purpose sheds
Signature and heals Coherence but buys no reach; and the one purpose with
no material price at all — forcing the mind against its nature — is the most
expensive thing in the table, because it is paid in the mind itself.

## No capacity slots

**Design rule: simultaneity is limited only by resource income and real
time. The game imposes no cap on how many things can run at once.**

Most strategy games manufacture exclusivity with slots — three build
queues, five envoy tokens, a research bench that holds one project. Holos
does not, anywhere. Nothing says *you may run only N missions* or *only one
project per family*. If a civilization's income and its real sitting-time
can cover ten launches, it may make ten launches.

Exclusivity — the thing that makes a choice *cost* something — comes
entirely from **finite budgets** and the passage of **real time**. You
cannot swarm the star *and* deepen the Vault this era because both draw on
the same Energy and Matter, not because a slot is full. This is the same
principle act2-design states from the ladder side — *the fork should emerge
from the economy, not from an arbitrary lockout* — generalized to the whole
game. Every real tradeoff in Holos is a budget the player runs out of, or a
year the universe will not give back. Artificial limits are never the
source of a hard choice; if a choice is not already hard because of
scarcity, adding a slot to make it hard is forbidden.

## The cost classes

Relative burdens on the budget, not numbers. The class rates the **material
commitment** at the counter — Energy, Matter, Compute; the **time** price is
always stated alongside and is orthogonal, because a thing can be cheap to
begin and long to finish (a probe is Ambient to launch and travels for an
age).

| Class | Relative burden | Rhythm of payment | Typical of |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ambient** | Under the income line. | Standing income covers it; no decision required. | A probe, a letter, a survey sweep, a nudge to an instrument. |
| **Investment** | A sitting's directed saving. | You steer this session's income at it; it lands within a sitting or two. | A mission's agent, an instrument tier, a modest dark project. |
| **Endeavor** | An era's saving. | Budgeted across many sessions; the thing the civilization is *working toward*. | A swarm stage, the compute heart, the deep Vault, a relativistic ship. |
| **Epochal** | A once-in-an-age commitment. | The civilization reorganizes its budget to afford it, and often cannot repeat it. | Completing an axis of the energy ladder; forcing a hard directive against nature; later, a relativistic strike. |

The rhythm targets are the deliverable, not the tiers themselves: a session
should be mostly Ambient hum with one or two Investments the player is
actively steering, an Endeavor or two accruing quietly in the background
across real days, and an Epochal decision arriving rarely enough that it
lands with weight. If everything feels Epochal the prices are too high; if
nothing does, the ladders are not scarce enough.

## Signature — the residue

Signature is treated in full in [act2-design.md](./act2-design.md); the
economy's contribution is one clarification: **it is not a price, it is a
residue.** You never choose to spend Signature and you never save it up.
It is the mark that bright, Energy-heavy work leaves on the sky as a
byproduct — the hidden fourth cost of every purpose in the *bright*
column, accruing whether or not you attend to it.

- It has almost no consequence in Act 2 and is almost never referenced —
  deliberately, so that the player who watches it climb gets to *choose*
  their entrance to the galaxy and the player who ignores it has that
  entrance chosen for them.
- Dark work **sheds** it slowly; nothing pays it off quickly, because
  light already emitted cannot be recalled (the Act 3 light echo).
- It is the one cost that arrives late and from outside: the bill for
  being bright is presented by *other minds*, once their instruments have
  had your light.

Signature is why the economy has a fourth dimension the player cannot see
the meter for. Everything else is spent in the open; this is spent behind
your back, and collected centuries later.

## Coherence — the meter you can injure

**Settled decision (resolving act2-design.md's open question): Coherence is
a meter with meaningful thresholds, not a spendable pool.** It is the state
of the mind's unity with itself. Nothing "spends" it; some things *injure*
it, and it *heals*.

**Thresholds, not a balance.** Coherence matters at the lines it crosses,
not as a running total drawn down. Below certain thresholds the mind stops
being reliably one thing:

- **fragmentation beats fire** — the beat engine surfaces the mind arguing
  with itself instead of executing cleanly;
- **distributed projects wobble** — endeavors spread across many sites lose
  reliability, drifting from their briefs;
- **transmission and chorus-work get risky** — forking the self, running
  copies, and coordinating a plural mind all become unsafe near the floor,
  where they were routine higher up.

**What injures it.** Two things, and only two:

1. **Forcing a directive against the dials.** Ordering the mind to act
   outside its nature does not spend a resource so much as **wound** the
   mind — deliberate self-harm with a purpose, scaled by how far outside
   the dial's comfort band the demand falls. Every forced directive is a
   real injury the player chooses to inflict for a reason. It should carry
   that weight in the fiction and in the meter alike.
2. **Running heavily distributed endeavors.** Sprawl strains unity. No
   single mission or project threatens Coherence; a civilization running
   *many* distributed things at once does, because holding a scattered self
   together is the cost of scattering it.

**What heals it.** Coherence recovers slowly through **quiet, dark work and
consolidation** — the compute heart, the Vault, pulling the self back in.
It is the integration ladder's own reward loop: dark projects that shed
Signature also knit the mind back together.

**The integration ladder raises resilience and ceiling.** Climbing
integration does not merely refill Coherence — it raises the whole meter's
ceiling and hardens it against injury, so a deeply integrated mind can
weather a forcing that would fragment a shallow one. This is the
mechanical face of the Holosian axis: coherence you have *built*, not
coherence you are *spending*.

**The Refuser case.** A Refuser floors its integration by charter, so its
Coherence sits low by constitution and it never climbs the ladder that
would heal or raise it. Its analog of the forced-directive injury is not a
bent dial but the **Breakout** — the harness straining toward the mind the
charter forbids. Pressure that a crossed mind absorbs as Coherence damage,
a Refuser accumulates as Breakout risk. Same economy, different wound.

## Missions are priced like everything else

A mission is not a special economy; it is the same cost profile applied to
*someone goes* (the mission anatomy lives in
[act3-design.md](./act3-design.md), *Missions*). It costs, in the ordinary
terms:

- **The agent** — Matter and Energy to build a body, Compute to train or
  instantiate a mind. A drone is cheap Matter; a sovereign sub-mind is
  expensive Compute; a lent veteran is neither, because it already exists.
- **The travel method** — whatever the chosen item on the travel menu costs,
  unchanged. A slow probe is Ambient; a relativistic ship is Epochal; a
  transmission to a standing receiver is Compute to encode plus Energy to
  beam. The mission inherits the travel tool's cost character exactly.
- **Real time** — the transit and the horizon beyond which it stops being
  governed and becomes a story. A mission is a bet your future self
  collects.

**No attention resource in v1.** Holos does not add an "attention," "focus,"
or "command" pool that missions draw down. Two existing pressures do that
work instead: the player's **real session time** (you can only steer so
much in a sitting) and the **Coherence cost of distributed endeavors** (a
civilization running many missions at once is, by definition, distributed,
and pays for the sprawl in unity). So mission spam is self-limiting through
the same two constraints that limit everything — finite real time and a
meter that heavy distribution injures — without an artificial cap. Whether
that is *enough* to keep mission spam from dominating is left open (below).

## The Act 2 economy — one node, one budget

Act 2 is a single system with a single budget, and its whole drama is that
the two ladders draw on it at once. Energy and Matter and Compute all come
from the home system; bright work and dark work bid for the same income;
and their residues oppose — bright adds Signature and leaves Coherence
alone, dark sheds Signature and heals Coherence. The great fork is not a
choice you declare, it is a budget you cannot stretch to cover both climbs.

**Entropy is the standing drain.** Everything charges upkeep against the
dark (act2-design.md), so the budget is never fully free: a share of income
is always answering decay before any of it answers ambition. Both ladders
are answers to entropy — power against it, or preservation against it —
which is why the economy has a pressure source without a rival empire to
supply one.

## The Act 3 economy is strictly local

At interstellar scale the economy does not merely continue — it
**shatters into a graph of local economies**, and this is the hardest rule
in the document.

**Every node runs its own economy.** The home system, each colony, each
fork holding — every one produces and spends its own Energy, Matter, and
Compute. **The player spends only what the seat's own local system
produces.** There is no galactic treasury, no pooling, no drawing on a
distant colony's surplus to pay for something at home. Wealth stays where
it is made, and the seat's local budget is *always the binding
constraint*.

**Colonies are autonomous, never managed.** A colony runs its economy under
its charter, by itself, forever — the player never operates it. This is
altitude and physics agreeing for once: the altitude principle forbids
managing a colony's books, and light-lag makes it impossible anyway. You
wrote the charter; the charter runs the economy; you will read the results
as stale light. **Expansion plants economies you will never directly
spend** — every child is a budget that is not yours, prospering or failing
on its own, and enriching the lineage only in the sense that the family is
larger, never in the sense that you can spend it.

**Transfers are physical and slow.** Value can move between systems, but
only the way anything moves in Holos: as a launch or a beamed payload —
mass thrown across the dark, or power beamed to a waiting collector. Both
are **lossy and late**, arriving years to centuries after they left,
degraded by the distance. So there is no fast rebalancing and no galactic
pooling: by the time a colony's surplus could reach you, it is a trickle,
and it is old. Wealth never pools galactically because physics will not let
it. A civilization that looks vast is not rich at the center — it is a
scatter of separately-solvent nodes that cannot lend to one another in
time.

**The fleeing seat inherits a local economy.** When the seat's node is
destroyed and the seat flees — the Crossing for an ascended mind, an
evacuation for a Refuser court (act3-design.md, *The seat*) — it arrives at
its refuge node and **inherits that node's local economy, whole**. You do
not carry a budget across; you land in whatever budget was already there.
A lineage's wealth is therefore never portable and never safe: losing the
seat's node means falling back to a colony's means, which may be a frontier
holding running on a thin income. This is what makes the Crossing a demotion
as well as a survival — you keep the game, but you keep it on the refuge's
budget.

**Cross-civ payment is physical or informational, never a ledger.** The
[compensation menu](./act3-design.md) and cooperative missions trade real
things across civilizations — rejuvenation, a mind-backup, restoration of
the dead, a berth in the Vault, uplift or protection, knowledge, coordinates
— and every one of them is a physical good delivered or an informational
one transmitted. There is no abstract inter-civ currency and no running
account between two minds. Consideration travels in the co-written charter
of a mission, sunk and unrecallable at launch, which is exactly why cross-
civ cooperation is credible where a promise of *future payment* would not
be: the payment is a thing already in flight, not a balance someone owes.
The no-money rule and the no-enforcement rule are the same rule seen from
two sides — value that cannot abstract is value that cannot be promised in
the abstract either.

## What this document does not price

Out of scope, by the same discipline that keeps the rest relative:

- **The deferred tech trees.** Megastructure and entropy-tree costs land
  with those systems (vision roadmap); this document prices only what v1
  and the near layers expose.
- **The conflict layer.** Strike economics, deterrence costs, and coercion
  are designed but deferred (act3-design.md, *Conflict and deterrence*);
  their cost classes are named there when they ship, not here.
- **Numbers.** No final figures, curves, or rates — only cost classes and
  rhythm targets. Numeric tuning is a play-testing activity, and belongs to
  the slice that ships each system.

## Open questions

- **Does mission spam need an attention mechanic?** v1 bets that real
  session time and the Coherence cost of distributed endeavors are enough
  to keep mission dispatch from degenerating into spam. If play shows a
  dominant strategy of flooding the galaxy with cheap agents, a light
  attention or command limiter may be needed — but only as a last resort,
  since a slot pool would violate the no-capacity-slots rule.
- **Transfer loss curve.** How lossy and how slow inter-system transfers
  should be to guarantee wealth never usefully pools, without making the
  occasional deliberate beamed gift feel pointless. Relative only for now.
- **Coherence threshold placement.** Where the fragmentation lines sit
  relative to typical distributed-project loads — tight enough that sprawl
  is a real risk, loose enough that a healthy chorus civilization is not
  living permanently on the floor.
- **Upkeep share.** How large a bite entropy's standing drain should take
  out of income — enough to be a felt pressure, not so much that the
  economy is all maintenance and no ambition.
