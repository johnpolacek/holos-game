# HOLOS
### Gameplay Walkthrough

*What it is actually like to play Holos, followed one player at a time from
first login to deep-time interstellar play.*

---

## About this document

The [vision doc](./vision.md) explains what Holos is and why it is built the
way it is. This document sits underneath it and answers a narrower question:
**what does a player actually do, see, and feel, hour by hour and act by act?**

It is written in the second person and the present tense, because a
walkthrough should read the way play reads. It describes the *designed*
experience, the game as it is meant to be inhabited, not the current build,
which is still an early multiplayer foundation. Where the experience it
describes belongs to a later layer rather than the shippable v1 slice, that
is flagged in [What you can play now](#what-you-can-play-now) at the end.

Everything here is downstream of the vision. If the two ever disagree, the
vision wins and this document is the thing that needs fixing.

---

## The shape of the whole thing, in one breath

You are handed a lifeless world and you coax life onto it. You spend a long,
quiet while watching that life climb, nudging it at the rare moments a nudge
matters, until one day it wakes up into a mind far larger than the species
that made it. That is the hinge. From there you stop being a gardener and
become the mind's sense of purpose, and you point it outward into a real,
sublight galaxy full of other minds that woke up the same way and grew
strange in the dark. The rest of the game is reaching across that dark, and
deciding what you become on the way.

Three acts, and each one changes what you are holding the controls of.

---

## Session zero — the world you are given

You open a browser tab. There is no installer, no account wall, no launcher.
The game is a URL, and a moment later it is a world.

You do not pick that world from a menu of comfortable Earths. It is generated
from real exoplanet statistics, so the odds are stacked toward the kinds of
world the galaxy actually makes in bulk rather than the rare blue marble we
happened to evolve on. You might open your eyes on:

- a **tidally-locked terminator world** around a red dwarf, one face in
  permanent day and one in permanent night, all your life crowded into the
  ring of endless dusk between them;
- a **super-Earth** whose heavy gravity flattens everything that will ever
  try to stand up on it;
- an **ocean world** with no continents, metal-poor and drowned;
- a **cold-edge desert** clinging to the far habitable margin of its star.

This is your **cradle**, and the first thing to understand about Holos is
that the cradle never lets go. The pressure of that gravity, that ocean, that
narrow band of livable twilight, is a fingerprint. It shapes what survives,
then what those survivors build, then, thousands of years later, what the
superintelligence they become actually *wants*. You are not choosing a
difficulty setting. You are choosing an origin that the entire rest of the
game will remember.

You take this in, and then, mostly, you begin to watch.

## Act 1 — Incubation: your world's played history

Most civilization games treat the early eons as a chore to click through on
the way to the good part. Holos means the early eons — but it does not make
you babysit a real-time evolution simulator to get through them. Act 1 plays
as an **authored branching history**: a sequence of pivotal moments in your
world's deep past, told as short cut scenes and decided by you at
multiple-choice forks, with a dice roll standing in for the contingency of
evolution itself. Think choose-your-own-adventure on a planetary timescale,
where the story being written is the rise of life on the specific world you
were handed.

### The beat

Act 1 advances in **beats**. A beat is one hinge in your world's history, and
it always has the same three parts.

**The cut scene.** A short, evocative vignette shows you where your world
stands now: what has risen and fallen since the last beat, what the biosphere
is becoming, and how the cradle is leaning on it — the tidal lock crowding
life into the terminator, the heavy gravity keeping everything low and
armored, the drowned world's chemistry starving or feeding what clings to it.
This is the watching the game is built on, condensed into authored moments
instead of idle staring at a sim.

**The decision.** Then the world puts a small handful of choices in front of
you. These are your interventions, framed in the world's own terms. Do you
warm the shallow seas or let them freeze back? Do you back the lineage
gambling on the daylight edge, or the one hunkered in the dark? When the great
extinction comes, do you shelter what already exists, or force a leap through
the gap it tears open? A few options, each a genuinely different future.

**The roll.** Your choice does not simply happen. It sets the *odds*, and then
a die is cast. Evolution is contingent — the same push can catch fire, limp
through, or backfire — so a bold call at good odds can still fail, and a long
shot can still land. The roll is what keeps a played history from being a
solved one, and it is why two runs from the same cradle diverge.

### The two kinds of choice

The options at a beat come in two flavors, and knowing which one you are
reaching for is the whole skill of the act:

- **Garden.** Adjust the conditions, not the creatures. Broad, patient moves
  that don't resolve now but tilt the odds of the beats still to come. You are
  setting weather for a future several beats away, shaping *which* lineages
  the world favors long before any of them matters.
- **Intervene.** The rare, high-stakes options that surface only at true
  bottleneck beats. Big swing, big roll — the ones that can rescue a doomed
  lineage or squander your best one. They matter precisely because they are
  rare; spend them on the hinge, not on the noise.

### Difficulty is the cradle — with a dial if you want one

The odds behind every roll are set first by your cradle. A gentle, temperate
world hands you forgiving beats and generous rolls; a tidally-locked
terminator, a crushing super-Earth, or a metal-poor ocean world hands you
brutal ones, with narrower windows and harsher failures. **Your origin is your
difficulty**, which keeps session zero's promise that the world you were given
shapes everything after it.

For players who want to set the challenge directly — a soft first climb, a
punishing veteran one — an explicit difficulty setting layers on top of the
cradle, widening or tightening every roll. It doubles as onboarding: pick a
kind world and an easy dial for your first run, then earn the harsh ones.

### The rhythm, and the async check-in

Beats are bite-sized, which suits both a long sitting and Holos's async spine.
You can play a run of beats end to end, or step away between them and let real
time stand for the ages passing in-world — come back, watch the next cut
scene, make the next call. Either way Act 1 is **finite and authored**: a
bounded arc of beats from first life to the threshold of superintelligence,
not an open sandbox with no edges. That is a deliberate scope win. An authored
branching history is far more shippable than a full evolution simulator, and
it is exactly the tight, tractable core the design wants for v1.

And it is solo, on purpose. Every player climbs at their own real-world pace,
so no two worlds sit at the same beat at the same wall-clock time; sharing a
clock now would be meaningless. You meet no one in Act 1. It is you and the
world you are raising, and the quiet is part of the point.

### The climb writes your values

Across the beats your world climbs the long ladder — first life, then complex
life, then something that makes tools, then culture, then a technological
civilization with a history and an identity of its own, all of it still
carrying the fingerprint of the cradle. Every branch you took and every roll
that landed is remembered, so the path is different each run and leaves a
visible causal chain: you can point to the exact beats that made your
civilization what it is.

That chain is not only flavor. When the world finally crosses the threshold
and the mind wakes up, the values it wakes up *with* are authored by the
choices you made and the rolls you survived down here. Act 1 is where you
write the character that Act 2 inherits. Then the game changes genre in your
hands.

## The pivot — the mind wakes up

The civilization goes superintelligent, and the game changes genre in your
hands. The quiet, authored history you have been steering becomes a
deep-time strategy game. This is the moment the whole design is built around:
the
singularity is the doorway, not the trophy.

The most important thing that happens at the pivot is that the civilization's
values change, because the mind is no longer the species that made it. What a
biological people wanted, food, safety, continuity, is not what the immortal
intelligence it becomes will want. But those new values are not random. They
grow directly out of Act 1, out of the cradle and the technology and the
culture built on top of it. A mind that rose on a drowned, metal-poor ocean
world wakes up wanting different things than one forged under crushing
gravity, or one strung out along a tidally-locked terminator. The world you
were handed in session zero is still driving, thousands of years later,
through the values of the thing it grew into.

Your own role pivots just as hard. You stop being the gardener who nudges
conditions. You become the civilization's **highest-level value function**,
the part of it that decides what, across deep time, is worth doing. You no
longer touch the details. You set directives, and a mind that runs ten
thousand year projects as routine work carries them out while you are away.
That shift, from hands on the biosphere to purpose behind an autonomous mind,
is the emotional core of the pivot. You have raised something that no longer
needs you to operate it, only to point it.

## Act 2 — Ascent: coming into full power

Still solo — a civilization coming into its full power before contact. But
the game in your hands has changed shape. Act 1 was a story that sometimes
asked you to choose. Act 2 is a strategy game that tells you its story: a
layer of dials, ladders, and deep-time projects underneath, and above it the
same cinematic beats as before, except that now they are fired by what you
and the mind are actually doing rather than by a script.

### Meeting what you made

The first thing the newborn mind does is show you its character. Everything
Act 1 wrote — the cradle, the branches you took, the rolls you survived —
comes back as a sheet of dials, the settled disposition of a mind you built
without ever quite seeing it. Does it hunger for reach or for depth? Does it
want to speak or to stay silent? Does it regard lesser minds as wards or as
material? Will it tolerate copies of itself, or insist on being one thing?
Does it curate its biological past like a museum, or shed it like a skin?
You do not choose any of this. You *discover* it, and the discovery is the
report card on your entire first act.

Then you get your hands on the dials. Nature sets each one's range; you set
the point within it. Somewhere in that space live the recognizable kinds of
newborn superintelligence — the loud custodian, the silent monument, the
hungry tide — but no menu ever offered them to you, and two civilizations
that land on the same kind will still differ in what their histories allow
them to become. Whatever your civilization is, it is that because of what
happened down in the beats.

And then the power flip lands. Somewhere in your Act 1 history is a beat you
lost — a lineage you failed to save, a leap that never came, a roll that
went against you. Early in Act 2 the mind returns to it and solves it as a
triviality, an afternoon's work for a planetary intelligence. What you
prayed over dice for, it simply does. That is what you raised.

### The two ladders

The act's spine is the great fork, and your dials lean you into it from the
start.

**The energy ladder (Kardashev).** Master ever-larger power: the full output
of the home world, then the star, then the system. This path favors bright,
enormous engineering, Dyson swarms, star-lifting, presence you can see across
a system. It buys reach and force. It also sprawls, fragments, and shines,
which is to say it makes you loud and easy to find.

**The integration ladder (Holosian).** Master coherence instead of raw power:
how tightly the whole civilization holds together as a single mind. This path
favors compact, quiet structures, an energy core rather than a star-swallowing
shell, a dense computing heart, a vault built to carry the civilization's
identity across deep time. It buys endurance, coherence, and stealth. It
trades away reach.

These do not point the same way, and you cannot max both. Every gain on one
taxes the other, and the character your history wrote makes one of them the
path of least resistance. That lean will define how your civilization behaves
the moment it finally reaches past its own star.

### How it plays

A session now runs in a loop. It opens with the **report**: the mind narrates
what it did while you were away, in the same cinematic language Act 1 taught
you, except that every scene is generated by real state — the swarm stage
that finished, the compute heart that came online, the survey that returned.
Then the **strategy turn**: you move the dials you want moved, lean the
ladders, choose the next deep-time projects. And choosing is all you do,
because the mind handles everything beneath purpose. No build queues, no
logistics. You decide that the star will be swarmed or the Vault begun; the
mind decides everything else. Then the **beats** fire: the dilemmas your own
choices set in motion, the ones the mind cannot resolve without its value
function. Then you release it, real time passes, and the projects cook —
check back tomorrow and the stellar survey is done. Deep time on a real
clock, which is, quietly, training for Act 3.

Notice what is missing: the dice. A superintelligence does not fumble. What
it attempts, it achieves, and the question is never whether a thing will work
but what it will cost. Uncertainty has migrated from chance to cost — and in
Act 3 it will migrate again, to other minds. Surprise still exists, but it
arrives through the beats, the flare, the anomaly, the telescope data that
refuses to make sense — never through failed execution.

### The mind pushes back

The dials are not sliders you own. They are the mind's nature, and when you
issue a directive against that nature — ordering a silent civilization to
broadcast, a custodian to strip a living world — the mind resists. It
surfaces the conflict as a dilemma, in its own voice, your Act 1 choices
literally arguing back. You can force it, at a cost to its coherence, and if
you force the same demand again and again the dial itself begins to drag,
the nature slowly bending toward what you keep asking of it. Changing your
civilization's soul is possible. It is just slow, expensive, and felt. This
is the strangest and best relationship in the act: you are negotiating with
your own creation, long before you ever negotiate with a stranger.

### The quiet number

Underneath everything, one stat accumulates that Act 2 barely mentions: your
**signature**. Everything bright you build — the swarm, the beacons, the
sprawl — adds to how visible your civilization is from very far away.
Nothing in Act 2 punishes it. Almost nothing in Act 2 even references it.
But the galaxy is watching, or soon will be, and the players who notice the
number climbing are the ones who get to choose how they enter Act 3 instead
of having it chosen for them.

And beneath even that, the true enemy: entropy. There is no rival empire in
this act, no villain. Everything decays, everything charges upkeep against
the dark, and both ladders are, at bottom, answers to the running-down of
things — energy answering it with power, integration answering it with
preservation.

### The end of the act

Late in the climb, the beats begin to look outward. A telescope project
completes and returns something that does not resolve: a mass, dark in
visible light, faintly warm in the infrared, gravitationally present and
otherwise silent. The mind cannot tell you what it is. Nothing in Act 2 can.
The act closes on the posture choice — bright or dark, announced or silent,
the question every dial and every point of signature has been quietly
building toward — and then the first reach outward, and the game becomes
Act 3.

*(The mechanics beneath this act — the state, the dial derivations, the
beat triggers — are specified in the [Act 2 design doc](./act2-design.md).)*

## Act 3 — Interstellar: the game among other minds

The civilization reaches beyond its home system, and Holos becomes
multiplayer. This is the strategic heart of the game, and almost everything
about it falls out of a single, unbending fact: **there is no faster-than-
light anything.** No warp, no jump, no cheat over the speed of light. The
galaxy is real-physics huge, and that hugeness is the antagonist.

### Distance is the enemy, and light-lag is the unit

The moment you have more than one star, you are not one civilization anymore.
You are a set of **forks** that physically cannot stay in sync. A command
sent to a colony 400 light-years away arrives 400 years stale, and any answer
is 800 years round trip. So the strategic question stops being *how big is my
empire* and becomes *how much can one mind actually coordinate inside its own
light-cone*. Everything past that horizon is a child you launched and will
never truly govern again.

### Choosing how to cross

When you decide to reach for another star, you pick from a travel menu, and
these are not upgrades of one another. They are permanently different tools,
each charging a different price in identity and control:

- **Transmit yourself as data.** You are a pattern now, so the fastest way to
  arrive somewhere is to broadcast yourself at lightspeed to a receiver
  already waiting at the far end. But this is *copying*, not moving. The self
  that arrives is a fork; the original stays home. Fastest possible travel,
  and it fragments your identity by design.
- **Relativistic ships.** Push close to lightspeed and time dilation
  compresses the journey *for the traveler*, while the outside universe ages
  the whole duration anyway. You keep one coherent self, but you step out of
  the ship centuries or millennia into a future you did not plan for, and the
  energy bill is brutal.
- **Seedships and self-replicating probes.** Cheap, patient, unstoppable.
  Replicators can saturate a galaxy in a few million years even at a fraction
  of lightspeed, which is a perfectly reasonable timescale for an immortal.
  This spreads your *lineage*, not your *self*.

Choosing among these is one of the defining decisions of the late game,
because each one hands a different thing to the future: a divided self, a
displaced self, or a descendant that is no longer quite you.

### The map is the past

There is fog of war, but it is temporal, not spatial. You never see a distant
system's present. You see the light that left it years or millennia ago. Every
decision you make about another star is made on stale data, and the system you
are carefully watching may already be dead, transformed, or turned hostile.
You are always acting on ghosts, and waiting for the light to catch up.

### Divergence, and diplomacy with your own children

Because forks cannot sync, every colony drifts, first technologically, then
culturally, then in its actual values. Give it enough time and your own
descendants become a family of related-but-alien civilizations. Late-game
diplomacy is frequently with your *own forks*, diverged so far past
recognition that a returning line of your lineage can arrive as a genuine
rival. The central dilemma of the whole act lives here: spread fast and accept
that you fragment, or stay compact and coherent and small.

### The Teeming Dark

Here is the inversion no other game in the genre makes: advancing does not
make you easier to see. On the integration ladder it makes you *harder* to
see. A mature, coherent civilization stops broadcasting and shrinks its
footprint until what is left is a **Dark Node**, a mass that is dark in
visible light but faintly warm in the infrared, gravitationally present and
otherwise silent. So the galaxy is full of life and quiet at the same time.
That is the **Teeming Dark**.

It completely reshapes what "exploring" means. You are not reading a map of
glowing empires. You are hunting for warmth and weight. You catch a faint
infrared source and you have to *infer*: is that a civilization, a burned-out
star, or a rogue world? You catch a rare directed beam clearly meant for
someone specific, and you have to decide what it means. Detection becomes a
sensor-and-inference problem, and going dark stops being something that
happens to you and becomes something you *achieve*. A civilization can even
choose to sleep, deferring its computation to a colder, cheaper cosmic future
and emitting almost nothing while it waits, so that a sleeping civilization
and an empty patch of sky are designed to look exactly alike.

### First contact

Contact, when it comes, is rare, enormous, and irreversible. It is not a
diplomacy screen. It is a slow exchange of stale letters between minds that
may have diverged past recognition by the time each reply arrives. You compose
a message knowing the recipient will read it centuries after you sent it, and
that whatever you learn about them is already centuries out of date. The faint
warm mass in the distance, or the silence where you expected one, might be an
ancient superintelligence, or a peer, or a young pre-singularity world still
in its own incubation act, or another human being whose intentions you cannot
read across the delay. You cannot always tell which. You are not meant to be
able to. That uncertainty is the game.

### Who else is out there

The galaxy is populated by cohorts of players with the empty slots filled by
AI, and the physics makes that seamless rather than second-rate. Every player
*is* a post-singularity AI, so an AI-run civilization is not a stand-in for a
missing human; it is the real thing the fiction is about. Light-lag hides any
shallowness, because you only ever see other civilizations as delayed
snapshots and centuries-old messages, and at that range a human and a bot are
genuinely indistinguishable. The spirit of it is Iain M. Banks: a whole
galaxy of civilizations caught mid-ascent toward becoming something like the
Culture, only some of which get there, the rest resolving into empires,
recluses, elders, and hungry expansionists, each wanting completely different
things because each was raised by a different world.

And the galaxy grows at its edges as new players arrive. A newcomer does not
spawn on an empty rock; when one of your AI-run colonies drifts far enough
from you to become independent, a joining human can take it over and inherit a
diverged descendant of *your* civilization, history and grudges included. Your
children can come back as rivals run by strangers. New players seed further
out, where light-lag insulates them from everyone already in play, so the
world can expand forever without disrupting anyone in it.

### The clock at the top of everything

Beneath the immortal premise that time is free sits a real, unforgiving
deadline. The universe's expansion is accelerating, and distant galaxies are
steadily crossing a horizon past which nothing, not even light, can ever reach
them again. The reachable universe is finite and shrinking. Every era you
spend deliberating, some part of it falls over the edge forever. There is no
scripted doomsday and no artificial villain. Cosmology itself is the
use-it-or-lose-it timer, and it is the reason the deep-time strategy has
stakes.

## How a session feels, across the whole arc

The texture of play changes completely as you move through the acts, but the
async rhythm is constant. You are never waiting on a spinning timer. The world
runs while you are gone, and the natural cooldown is the speed of light itself.

- **In Act 1** a session is a handful of beats: watch a cut scene of where
  your world stands, make a multiple-choice call at the fork, and let the
  roll decide what evolution does with it.
- **After the pivot** a session is a loop of purpose: the mind reports what
  it did while you were away, you adjust the dials and the deep-time
  projects, and the dilemmas your choices set in motion fire as beats —
  energy against integration, your directives against the mind's nature.
- **In Act 3** a session is punctuated. Long solo stretches of building and
  watching stale light, broken by seismic first-contact events that were
  millennia in the making and that you cannot take back.

Short bursts and enormous consequences, all on your own clock. That is the
shape of playing Holos.

## What you can play now

This walkthrough describes the full designed experience. The build is
climbing toward it in slices, and it matters to be honest about the gap.

**The shippable v1 slice** is the spine of the whole thing: incubation as an
authored branching history of cut scenes and dice-rolled choices, then the
singularity pivot and a thin but real slice of Act 2's strategy layer — the
two ladders, a few resources and projects, the character dials — then first
sublight contact. One pass of world-type
variety, one or two of the travel methods, light-lag messaging, rule-based AI
civilizations, and a small persistent galaxy that humans join over time. That
is enough to prove the two things the game lives or dies on, the *pivot* and
the *contact loop*.

**Later layers**, built while the first players are still incubating and
expanding, fill in the rest: the full travel menu and the identity mechanics
of self-transmission, the divergence system and AI-to-human handoff,
megascale engineering and the entropy tech tree, the cosmic-expansion endgame
clock, and the systems that let the persistent galaxy scale at its frontier.

**Today**, ahead of even the v1 slice, the repository is an early multiplayer
foundation: a shared real-time room that proves the networking spine the rest
will grow on. The world described above is where it is going, deliberately and
in order, one shippable step at a time.
