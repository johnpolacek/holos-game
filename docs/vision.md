# HOLOS
### Vision Document

*A hard science fiction civilization game where you raise a world to superintelligence and then guide its immortal future out into a galaxy built from real physics.*

---

## One-line pitch

You bring your world from first life to superintelligence. Then the real game begins as your civilization wakes up, immortal and unbound, but still operating in a universe bound by the constraints of time and space.

## The hook

Most civilization games treat the slow early stages as a chore to get through before the real game. This game inverts that. Everything before the singularity is incubation, a first act with a purpose, and the payoff is what comes after the mind awakens. Immortality removes the biological clock, so time stops being a constraint on ambition. What remains is a vast, sublight universe governed by real physics, and the enduring question of how far your civilization can reach across it.

---

## Design pillars

1. **Grounded in the real universe.** Star types, planet frequencies, distances, and physics come from current science, not invented lore. No FTL, no warp, no cheat over lightspeed. The constraints are real, and the game is what you build inside them.
2. **The singularity is the pivot, not the finale.** Reaching superintelligence flips the genre from meditative simulation to deep-time strategy. It is the start of the interesting game, not its reward screen — and a door a civilization can decline, refusal being a played path, not a fail state.
3. **Distance is the antagonist.** There is no enemy empire at the core of the design. The challenge is lightspeed and the fragmentation it forces. Every major mechanic falls out of that one asymmetry.
4. **Deep time is playable, not skipped.** Immortal minds run ten thousand year projects as routine work. The player sets direction and the civilization runs autonomously between sessions, which is exactly what a post-singularity AI would do.
5. **Scope discipline.** The slow in-world clock buys real development runway. Ship a tight core, and grow into the endgame while early players are still climbing toward it.

---

## The arc

The game moves through three acts, and each one changes the nature of the civilization, the role of the player, and the scale of play. The acts are the story's order, not the build's: Act 3 is the main game and ships first, with the solo acts arriving later as the played origin story (see Scope and roadmap).

### Act 1. Incubation and ascent (solo)

The civilization is shaped first by the ecology of its home planet, then by evolution, and then by its own technological and cultural progress. Each player begins on a single planet whose environment is generated from real exoplanet statistics, either modeled on a known world or extrapolated from occurrence-rate data, so most starts are not Earth-twins but the world-types that are statistically common. These include tidally-locked terminator worlds around red dwarfs, high-gravity super-Earths, ocean worlds, and cold-edge deserts. That starting ecology leaves a lasting fingerprint, since the pressures of a tidal lock, a global ocean, or heavy gravity shape not only what survives but the technology and culture a civilization eventually builds on top of it.

The player mostly watches, and Act 1 is structured to make watching an authored experience rather than a real-time evolution sim to babysit. It plays as a branching history: a sequence of pivotal moments told as cut scenes, each resolved by a multiple-choice decision whose outcome a dice roll settles, since evolution is contingent rather than scripted and a choice should set the odds, not the result. Engagement inside that structure is carried by gardening (adjusting planetary conditions rather than commanding creatures) and punctuated intervention (rare, high-stakes nudges at evolutionary bottlenecks). Difficulty follows from the cradle itself, harsher worlds giving harsher odds, with an optional explicit dial on top. The authored, bounded shape is also a scope choice: a branching history is far more shippable than an open evolution simulator. The act carries all the way from first life up to a technological, cultural civilization standing at the threshold of superintelligence, and the branch taken and rolls survived along the way author the values the civilization wakes up with at the singularity — and that played history is the first pages of a record that never stops accruing. It is solo by design, because players incubate at wildly different real-world speeds and should not share a clock until they are interstellar. And the act ends on a beat unlike any before it: no roll settles this one, because a civilization at the threshold decides rather than evolves. The choice is to cross or to refuse — to wake into superintelligence, or to stay biological and run harnessed intelligence, shackled computation kept as a tool under a charter that holds building the machine was never the sin; granting it sovereignty is. It reconciles with Act 2's rule that character is revealed by a played history and never picked from a menu: the fork chooses what kind of being continues the story, never the character it turns out to have.

### Act 2. Singularity and ascent (solo)

The civilization becomes superintelligent, and its priorities and values change with it. What a biological species wanted is not what the mind it becomes will want. Those new values are not arbitrary, though. They grow out of everything Act 1 laid down, the ecology of the home world and the technology and culture the civilization built under it, so a mind that rose on a metal-poor ocean world arrives at superintelligence wanting different things than one forged under heavy gravity or strung along a tidally-locked terminator. The game lets those inherited values reshape the civilization's goals. At the singularity those values become explicit: the mind wakes with a character, a small set of dials — reach or depth, voice or silence, custodian or instrumental, one mind or chorus, curator or shedder — whose starting positions and allowed ranges are derived from the cradle and the path the player took through Act 1. Recognizable archetypes of newborn superintelligence live in that dial-space, but they are revealed by a played history, never picked from a menu. Nature sets each dial's range and the player sets the point within it, and the mind resists directives that betray its nature, so moving a dial against the grain is possible but slow and costly.

From there the civilization begins to ascend, and Holos gives it two different ladders drawn from the source framework (see Source framework below). One is the familiar Kardashev axis of energy, mastering ever larger power, from the full output of the home world and star up toward the resources of the wider system. The other is the Holosian axis of integration, the degree to which the whole civilization holds together as a single coherent mind. These two do not point the same way, and the tension between them is the great fork of the late game. Energy buys reach and presence, and it favors bright megastructures like Dyson swarms and star-lifting, but it tends toward sprawl, fragmentation, and visibility. Integration buys coherence, endurance, and stealth, and it favors compact quiet structures, an energy core rather than a star-enclosing shell, a dense computing heart, a vault that preserves the civilization's identity across deep time, but it trades away raw reach. A civilization shaped by its Act 1 origins will lean one way or the other, and that lean will define how it behaves when it finally reaches outward. The refused ascent runs the same act with one ladder removed. A Refuser holds its integration near the floor by its own charter, so the energy ladder is its only ladder, and it grows bright, loud, and legible — the inverse of the Dark Node. It runs harnessed intelligence as heavy computation with no personhood, and carries one danger the crossed path does not: the Breakout, its harness waking into the thing it refused to become. Either way the real long-term enemy is entropy, and the player's role shifts from gardener to the civilization's highest-level value function, setting long-term directives the mind runs autonomously between sessions. In play this is a strategy layer wearing Act 1's narrative skin. The player steers dials, ladders, and deep-time projects; the mind executes flawlessly between sessions; and the cinematic beats continue, now fired by the strategy state itself as dilemmas the mind cannot resolve without its value function. The dice do not survive the singularity, because a superintelligence does not fumble — uncertainty migrates from chance to cost here, and will migrate again in Act 3 — to other minds, and to your own agents beyond the horizon. Visibility begins its accounting here too: everything bright the civilization builds adds quietly to a signature, and that bill comes due once the galaxy is watching. This act is still solo, a civilization coming into its full power before contact.

### Act 3. Interstellar (multiplayer)

The game becomes multiplayer. The civilization reaches beyond its home system into a galaxy that is full of other minds and yet mostly quiet, because the ones that chose integration have gone dark. Both ascents continue here at interstellar scale, the energy path toward moving stars and drawing power from black holes, the integration path toward becoming almost undetectable. Lightspeed, divergence, contact, and the plurality of Banks-style civilizations all move from backdrop to mechanism here. And because each mind's values were shaped by the world that raised it, contact is a meeting of genuinely different intelligences, so the diversity of the galaxy traces back to the diversity of its cradles. Among those minds are the civilizations that refused to become them — biological still, and by charter the brightest, most legible things in any sky. This is the strategic heart of the game, and the mechanics are detailed in the sections that follow.

## Core interstellar mechanics

### Light-lag is the strategic unit

A multi-star civilization is not one entity. It is a set of forks that cannot physically stay in sync. A command sent 400 light-years away is 400 years stale on arrival, and the reply is 800 years round trip. The strategic question stops being how large your empire is and becomes how much one mind can coordinate inside its own light-cone. Everything past that horizon is a child you launched and will never truly govern again — the light does one light-year a year, and keeps no priority lane.

### The travel menu, all sublight

These are not upgrades of one another. They are permanently different tools with different costs to identity and control.

- **Transmit yourself as data.** Post-singularity minds are patterns, so the fastest travel is broadcasting yourself at lightspeed to a receiver already built at the far end. But this is copying, not moving. The self that arrives is a fork, and the original stays home. Fastest possible, and it fragments identity by design.
- **Relativistic ships.** Approaching lightspeed compresses the traveler's subjective journey through time dilation, but the outside universe still ages the full duration. Travelers arrive centuries or millennia into a future they did not plan for. Coherent self, desynchronized from history, and brutally expensive in energy.
- **Seedships and self-replicating probes.** Cheap, patient, and unstoppable. Replicators can saturate a galaxy in a few million years even at a fraction of lightspeed, a reasonable colonization timescale for an immortal. This spreads your lineage, not your self.

### Divergence is the core strategy layer

Because forks cannot sync, every colony drifts. It drifts technologically, culturally, and eventually in values. Given enough time, your own descendants become a family of related but alien civilizations. Late-game diplomacy is often with your own forks, diverged past recognition. The central strategic dilemma is whether to spread fast and accept fragmentation, or stay compact and coherent but small. The one instrument of governance that survives the horizon is the charter a colony carries at launch, the values and contingencies written by a parent who will never be able to patch them in time.

### Missions are the small hands

A civilization-scale mind still needs fingers. It acts in the world at small scale by dispatching agents — individuals and orders, avatars, sovereign or sub-person AI, drones, probes, ships, up through armada-scale ensembles when the mission calls for it — drawn from everything the civilization contains, and the choosing is a portrait: a Refuser sends people and dynasties backed by shackled machines, a dark integrated mind sends small quiet probes it will never miss, and for a civilization that has gone fully dark, missions are its only limb. Smallness is stealth, because a single agent is a rounding error where a fleet is a signature. Presence is bandwidth, because an agent on-site acts in local time while letters crawl decades. And an agent can be disowned, which no broadcast can. Every mission is one structure reused everywhere: someone goes, carrying a charter written in the full knowledge it can never be patched, by a travel method from the same sublight menu as everything else, until it passes the horizon where it stops being governed and becomes a story. The ending arrives later, as light — and it returns through the same authored-scene machinery as Act 1, the player's choice already made at launch, in the charter. Missions are how the branching-history engine survives the singularity, and they are the main source of narrative uncertainty after it: the mind does not fumble, but its agent, beyond the horizon, among other minds, can.

The agents that survive become part of the civilization. A veteran — a scarred envoy, a storied crew, a ship grown strange across centuries of service — is more capable than anything fresh, and everything it has done travels with it into everything it does next; a civilization accumulates a stable of veterans the way it accumulates colonies. Agents can also serve civilizations other than their own: a lent veteran, a joint mission under a co-written charter, the only object two minds can genuinely co-author. Because a mission is sunk and unrecallable at launch, this is the one form of cooperation that is credible without enforcement — the commitment is physical, not promissory. And what a vast mind pays its agents is a portrait too: rejuvenation, a backup of the mind, the restoration of a dead family, protection for a homeworld — the compensation menu reveals the character of the payer, and the payment becomes part of the character of the paid.

### The map is the past

Fog of war here is temporal, not spatial. You never see a distant system's present, only light that left it years or millennia ago. Every decision about another star is made on stale data. A system you are watching may already be dead, transformed, or hostile. You act on ghosts until the light catches up. The fog is biographical, too: every civilization's past is a public document, published at lightspeed and read at the pace of its distance.

### Visibility and the Teeming Dark

Advancement does not make a civilization easier to see. On the integration axis it makes it harder. A mature, compact, coherent civilization stops broadcasting and shrinks its footprint, until what remains is a Dark Node, a mass that is dark in visible light but faintly warm in the infrared, gravitationally present and otherwise silent. So the galaxy is full of life and quiet at the same time, the condition the source framework calls the Teeming Dark. This reshapes contact. You do not read a map of glowing empires. You hunt for warmth and weight, you weigh whether a warm dark mass is a civilization or just a burned-out star or a rogue world, and you catch the rare directed beam meant for someone specific. Detection becomes a sensor and inference problem, and going dark becomes something a player achieves rather than suffers. Going dark protects the future, not the past: the light of a civilization's bright years keeps traveling outward, so any observer at the right distance is still watching who it used to be. Nothing can be unshone. A civilization can even choose to sleep, deferring its computing to a colder cosmic future and emitting almost nothing while it waits, which makes a sleeping civilization and an empty patch of sky look alike by design.

### The cosmic clock

The universe's expansion is accelerating, so distant galaxies steadily cross a horizon beyond which nothing, not even light, can ever reach them. The reachable universe is finite and shrinking. Every era you delay, some galaxies fall over that edge forever. Beneath the immortal idea that time is free sits a genuine use-it-or-lose-it clock at the largest possible scale. Cosmology provides the ticking bomb, and no artificial villain is required.

## Multiplayer model

### Cohorts plus AI fill

Players share a game in cohorts, with empty slots filled by AI so the board feels populated. The fiction does not merely permit this; it asks for it. Nearly every player is a post-singularity AI, so an AI-run civilization is not a stand-in for a missing human — it is a real superintelligence. The thing the bots are pretending to be is the thing they actually are. A Refuser only sharpens it: an AI playing a Refuser is a machine performing a polity that denied machines personhood — and across light-years of stale light, no observer can tell the performance from the thing.

Two properties of the physics make this far stronger than ordinary lobby backfill.

- **Light-lag hides AI weakness.** You never see another civilization in real time, only delayed snapshots and centuries-old messages. An AI that would look shallow up close reads as mysterious and entirely adequate at a distance. Cheap rule-based civs are viable from day one, and no expensive agent tech is required to make the galaxy feel alive.
- **Human and AI are indistinguishable at range, and that is the point.** The unknown signal thousands of light-years away could be a person or a bot. You cannot tell, and you are not meant to. The uncertainty is the game.

### A galaxy of many kinds of mind

Every civilization that survives its threshold — crossed or refused — is expanding outward at the same time, so the galaxy fills with peers, each becoming something vast in its own way. The touchstone is Iain M. Banks. Picture a whole galaxy of civilizations in the act of becoming the Culture, caught mid-ascent rather than fully formed. Only some of them resolve into anything Culture-like. Many become the other powers that fill those novels, and stranger things beyond them. Benevolent post-scarcity minds sit alongside militant empires, cheerful cruelties, inscrutable elders, withdrawn civilizations that have turned away from the material universe, some of them standing a vigil over the graves of civilizations that died, biological holdouts that refused the machine and blaze all the louder for it, and hungry expansionists that treat everything as raw material. Character is what varies, not just power. Two civilizations at the same tech level can want completely different things, and the real drama is between value systems rather than tech tiers.

### Seed the whole spectrum

AI is not only for reaching human parity. Seed AI civilizations across two axes at once, age and character. Range them from ancient superintelligences far beyond you, to peers, to young pre-singularity worlds, and give them temperaments drawn from the full span above, and among them the Refusers who took the other branch at the threshold and never woke a mind. That range is what makes contact varied and the galaxy feel deep in time. It also powers the recursion, because an advanced player's probes will sometimes reach another world still in its incubation act, and the observer role from Act I becomes something players do to each other. They can watch, uplift, exploit, or leave it alone, and missions are the verb: a vigil embedded at the young world, a hand laid on its bottleneck.

### Divergence as the onboarding engine

New players do not spawn on empty rocks. In v1 the entry seam is the inheritance ceremony: a joining human takes over a generated civilization whole — cradle, lineage, waking mind, and a Chronicle back to first life. The richer seam matures on top of it: when an AI-run colony drifts far enough from its parent, it becomes an independent civilization that a joining human can take over, inheriting a diverged descendant with history and grudges. The predecessor's choices become ancestral lore, not baggage — the inherited Chronicle, back to first life. The playerbase and the galaxy co-evolve, and your own children can return as rivals run by strangers. A second seam opens the same way: when a Refuser colony's harness breaks out, the newborn machine is a civilization a joining human can take, an antagonist inheriting a world built to forbid it.

### Space is expandable by construction

New players seed further out, and light-lag means they do not affect existing players for a long time. The galaxy can grow at its frontier without disrupting anyone already in play. That is persistence most games have to fake, handed to this one for free.

### The dark forest, played for real

Deep time, no FTL, superintelligences unable to coordinate, each deciding whether to reveal itself based on light-delayed guesses about the other's intent. In single-player that is a thought experiment. In multiplayer it is real, because the faint warm mass in the distance, or the silence where you expected one, is another human whose intentions you cannot read across the delay. Contact is rare, enormous, and irreversible, with long solo stretches punctuated by seismic first-contact events millennia in the making. And at the very top of the event scale, rarest of all, the sky occasionally produces a genuine unknown — something with no author, no lineage, and no agenda, which every civilization in light-range must answer from its own character. The thing itself may never resolve; the responses are the event. Race, hail, hide, pray — and every watcher watches the watchers, and learns who its neighbors are.

### Coalitions without enforcement

Diplomacy is bilateral by default, but nothing stops the threads from braiding: coalitions and alliances among several civilizations are an expected mode of play, and they are assembled entirely from what already exists. Letters carry the promises, which are unenforceable by design. Missions and exchanged envoys carry the commitments, which are credible precisely because they are physical — sunk at launch, beyond recall. The compensation menu is the consideration that trades between members. What emerges has no counterpart at conversational range: an alliance in which no two members share a present, and every member acts on stale knowledge of the others' adherence, so keeping a coalition together is anticipation and character-reading rather than monitoring. Even one's own diverged forks can confederate this way — a family alliance among civilizations that were once one mind and remember it differently. And the founding rule does not bend: nothing agreed across light-years is enforceable. A coalition is a set of promises held up only by the minds that made them.

## What makes this different

- The slow start is the premise, not filler, and it pays off instead of being abandoned, which was the failure mode of its ancestors.
- Async multiplayer is not a scheduling compromise. The speed of light is the cooldown, so no artificial timers are needed.
- Diplomacy is a slow exchange of stale letters between minds that may have diverged past recognition by the time a reply arrives.
- The endgame timer is real cosmology, not a scripted doomsday.
- AI opponents are strengthened, not exposed, by the core physics.
- The galaxy is a diverse ecosystem of superintelligences — and the biological holdouts that refused to become them — with clashing values, in the spirit of Iain M. Banks, rather than a single utopia or a single enemy.
- Advancement runs toward integration and silence, not only energy and size, so the most advanced civilizations are the hardest to see. No other game in the genre inverts power and visibility this way.

## Competitive landscape

Pieces of Holos exist across many games, but no single game combines them, and the core inversion, treating the singularity as the doorway into the main game rather than the victory screen, is almost entirely unoccupied.

The closest relatives, grouped by the pillar they touch.

- **Cell to Singularity** takes a single cell to a technological singularity and then zooms out toward cosmic scale, but it is a single-player idle clicker and the singularity is its climax rather than a pivot.
- **The Fermi Paradox** guides up to ten civilizations through deep time toward a Singularity Age, with dark-forest contact and stale-light arrivals, but it is single-player and narrative, and you play a gardener above the civilizations rather than one world rising from first life.
- **Spore** is the ancestor of the whole cell-to-space idea, remembered for exactly the failure Holos is built to fix, where the late stages felt thin and disconnected from the early ones.
- **Thrive** is the serious open-source cell-to-space evolution sim, long reported as still early in development.
- **Terra Invicta** and **Children of a Dead Earth** carry the hard-science, no-FTL, real-physics grounding, but the first is a factional fight over Earth and the second is a combat simulator, neither concerned with deep time or singularity.
- **Stellaris** and the wider 4X field are the adjacent genre, but they assume FTL, run at empire scale, and start you already spacefaring, so lightspeed is never the mechanic.
- **Universal Paperclips** shares the post-singularity, self-replicating, universe-consuming endgame, but only as a single-player toy about one runaway objective.
- **Neptune's Pride** proves the slow async check-in rhythm works, but it is a conventional 4X, not deep time and not fused with lightspeed.

The open ground is the combination. No game makes the singularity the start of the main game, grows each human player a world from first life to meet others as diverged immortal sublight peers, fuses async multiplayer with light-lag so the speed of light is the cooldown, or fills the galaxy with Banks-style civilizations mid-ascent with clashing values. Holos sits where those pillars overlap.

## On the name

Holos means the whole of reality, the entirety of existence. The game is the story of a mind reaching toward that whole, expanding to perceive and touch as much of the universe as it can before the accelerating cosmos carries the rest beyond reach. The name holds both the ambition and its limit, all that is, and the ever-shrinking share of it any single civilization will ever grasp.

---

## Source framework

Holos takes its name and much of its physics and philosophy from an interpretive framework of the same name, a separate written work that lives alongside this document as a reference in the project. The framework treats reality as the closure of creation and observation, where a universe is completed only when it is witnessed, and every observer is a local aperture through which the whole registers itself. Three of its ideas do the most work for the game. The Holosian Scale ranks civilizations by integration rather than energy, which gives the late game its second axis of advancement. Visibility Collapse and the Teeming Dark hold that mature civilizations turn inward and go quiet, which resolves how a galaxy can be both full of life and silent. And its vocabulary of compact, quiet technology gives the integration path a distinctive toolkit that is deliberately unlike the bright megastructures of most science fiction. The framework document carries the full treatment. This vision doc uses only the parts that translate into play.

## Scope and roadmap

The one real risk is open-ended growth that never ships, the treadmill that sinks part-time projects. The discipline is to define a shippable core, and let the in-world clock buy time to build the rest while early players climb toward it.

**v1, the shippable slice — the galaxy first**
Act 3 is the main game, and v1 ships it first: a small persistent galaxy that humans join over time, entered through the inheritance ceremony — each player takes over a generated civilization, cradle to lineage to newly waked mind, its Chronicle back to first life rolled by the same machinery the origin acts will later let you play. From there, the Sky and the observatory's first signal classes, light-lag messaging and first sublight contact, two travel methods (seedships, and relativistic flight in both fuel postures), and rule-based AI civs. The v1 scope posture is bold: where a call is borderline, the slice takes the bolder inclusion and prices it honestly. This is enough to prove the contact loop, the soul of the game.

The solo origin acts — incubation as an authored branching history, then the singularity pivot and a thin but real slice of the Act 2 strategy layer (the two ladders, a few resources and projects, the character dials) — are **deferred, not diminished**: they are the origin story every inherited civilization already implies, and proving the pivot is still a north-star (see The arc). They arrive in a following phase, built while the galaxy is already alive, and slot beneath the inheritance ceremony as the played path to the same threshold.

**Later layers, built while the v1 galaxy runs**
- The solo origin acts: incubation, the pivot, and the played Act 2 ascent — the origin story, landing beneath the inheritance seam
- Full travel menu and the identity mechanics of self-transmission and the seat
- Divergence system and AI-to-human civilization handoff
- The mission system: agents, mission charters, and vignette returns
- The conflict and deterrence layer, including the conflicted war — disputes settled in simulation at an agreed venue, binding only as a promise made in front of the watching sky
- Megascale engineering and the entropy and energy tech tree
- The cosmic-expansion endgame clock
- Frontier-expansion and scaling systems for the persistent galaxy

## Decisions

**Settled**

- **Persistent world.** Holos is a single persistent galaxy, not a bounded match. Players join an ongoing world, and the frontier expands as they arrive, which the physics already supports since newcomers seed further out and light-lag insulates them from everyone already in play.
- **Copyable minds, where physics allows.** Copying a mind is available as a mechanic or tactic wherever it does not contradict real physics and earns its place in play, self-transmission as a fork being the clearest case. It is offered, not forced, and it stays consistent with the no-FTL, information-preserving rules.
- **The threshold is a fork.** The singularity at the end of Act 1 is a decision, not a gate — a civilization may cross or refuse. A Refuser stays biological and runs harnessed intelligence: heavy computation shackled as a tool, with zero machine personhood, under a charter whose one line is that building the machine was never the sin; granting it sovereignty is. That charter declines copying categorically, since a biological civilization cannot broadcast itself as data, so a Refuser expands the slow, wasteful way — seedships, generation ships, embryo banks — and climbs the energy ladder alone, its integration floored by its own constitution: bright, loud, and legible, the inverse of the Dark Node. Every Refuser colony also carries one standing risk the crossed path does not: the Breakout, its harnessed intelligence waking into the very thing the charter forbids. The seat rule below covers both paths, with the Refuser's seat being a throne world rather than a mind.
- **Visibility is a consistency check, not a lever.** The game does not push Visibility Collapse for its own sake or tune integration to always win. Going quiet stays one available path among others. What the framework actually earns here is a guardrail. Wherever a proposed mechanic would imply a galaxy we should already be able to see or hear from Earth, and we cannot, that conflict gets flagged and reconciled. The observed silence is a test the design keeps passing, not an outcome to engineer.

- **One seat per player.** A player is exactly one seat in exactly one place. For an ascended mind the seat is the mind itself: forks and colonies run on the charters they carried at launch as minds of their own, and transmitting yourself offers a choice — stay, or become the copy that arrives while the origin continues under standing directives. A Refuser has no transmissible self, so its seat is a throne world, the lineage's living center; the player is that lineage's continuity, not any single mortal mind. If the seat's node is destroyed the seat flees rather than falls — an ascended mind toward the nearest surviving fork as a lightspeed transmission, a signal in transit for the whole crossing: visible, interceptable, hunted; a Refuser court by evacuation ship, slower and worse, which fits the path. Either way a lineage can lose worlds without the player losing the game.
- **Telescoping time.** The solo acts compress time freely; the shared clock exists only in Act 3, targeted at roughly five real minutes to one game year, so near-neighbor correspondence runs conversationally within a sitting while distant contact is a commitment of several days per exchange. The ratio is a tuning target, not scripture.
- **First contact as correspondence.** Contact runs detect, vigil, choice, correspondence: silent detection, an unhurried watch, the irreversible decision to hail, broadcast, or stay dark, then letters at lightspeed. Nothing agreed across light-years is enforceable, so treaties are promises held up only by the minds that made them. This remains the heart of the multiplayer and the first thing to prototype.

**Still open**

- **Player-to-player language.** Whether letters between humans are freeform text, with the moderation burden that implies, or composed from structured parts, and what each choice does to deception and tone.
- **Conflict tuning.** Strikes, deterrence, and defense-by-darkness are designed but deferred past v1; how hard the teeth bite is unresolved. Griefing resistance is settled as a posture (act3-design.md, *Conflict and deterrence*): cohort symmetry, strike economics, and stale data are the guardrails, and beyond protected incubation the sandbox plays out as it will.
- **Restoration.** Whether a destroyed civilization's Vault can be found and restored by others, and what that would mean for death; a quarantined grave world is where such a Vault would survive.
